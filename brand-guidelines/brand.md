# Mattermost brand

The source of truth for what Mattermost is, who it serves, and how it should come across in writing. Use this with [voice-and-tone.md](voice-and-tone.md) and [lexicon.md](lexicon.md) when writing or reviewing any external copy.

## What Mattermost is

Mattermost is a secure, open-source collaboration platform for technical teams doing mission-critical work. It runs as self-hosted software you control, or in a managed cloud, and brings messaging, workflows, and integrations into one place that meets strict security and data-residency requirements.

One-line description we stand behind:

> Mattermost is the secure, self-hostable collaboration platform for technical teams.

## Mission

Make it possible for teams to collaborate on their most sensitive, highest-stakes work without giving up control of their data. We believe the tools that run critical operations should be open, inspectable, and deployable on infrastructure the customer owns.

## Who we serve

Write for these people. They are technical, busy, and skeptical of marketing.

- **Developers and platform engineers** who want collaboration wired into their toolchain (CI/CD, Git, incident tooling).
- **Security, operations, and SRE teams** running DevSecOps and incident response where minutes matter.
- **IT and service-desk teams** that need request and approval workflows inside chat.
- **Administrators in regulated and high-security environments** (government, defense, finance, critical infrastructure) who require self-hosting, air-gapped deployments, and detailed access control.

## What we make

- **Channels** for real-time and threaded team messaging.
- **Playbooks** for repeatable, checklist-driven workflows like incident response.
- **Integrations and a plugin framework** for connecting the tools teams already use.
- **Self-hosted and Cloud deployment options**, with a community edition and commercial editions.

The product is built on Go (server) and React (web app), and is developed in the open.

## Brand attributes

Every piece of copy should reflect these. Each lists how it shows up and how it fails.

### Open
- **Do:** Reference the open-source project, transparency, and community honestly. Link to source, docs, or issues when useful.
- **Don't:** Treat "open source" as a slogan with nothing behind it.

### Secure
- **Do:** Talk about security as a property of the architecture: self-hosting, access control, data residency, deployment in restricted environments.
- **Don't:** Sell with fear. No threat-mongering, no invented breach statistics, no "hackers are coming" framing.

### Trustworthy
- **Do:** Be precise and verifiable. Prefer concrete capabilities over adjectives. State limits and requirements (license, configuration, edition).
- **Don't:** Overpromise, imply certifications we don't name, or use absolutes like "unhackable" or "100% secure."

### Technical and credible
- **Do:** Write to a technical reader. Use correct terms, real workflows, and accurate architecture.
- **Don't:** Dumb it down into vague business-speak, or dress it up with undefined buzzwords.

### Clear
- **Do:** Lead with the point. Short sentences. Plain words. Name the feature, role, or workflow directly.
- **Don't:** Bury meaning in hype, hedging, or filler.

### In control (data sovereignty)
- **Do:** Emphasize that customers own and control their data and deployment.
- **Don't:** Contradict this with cloud-only or lock-in framing.

## Anti-brand: what we are not

We are not a consumer chat app, and we don't sound like one. Avoid:

- Hype and superlatives ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "next-generation," "world's best").
- Fear-based security marketing.
- Growth-hack urgency ("Act now," "Don't miss out," fake scarcity).
- Vague enterprise filler ("synergy," "seamless," "frictionless," "supercharge," "unlock") used as a substitute for a real claim.
- Emoji-heavy, exclamation-heavy hype.
- Claims we can't back: specific numbers, certifications, or comparisons without a source.

## How we talk about competitors

Compete on substance: open source, self-hosting, security, and control. State our differentiators factually. Do not name and disparage competitors, and do not make unverifiable "better than X" claims.

## Quick gut check

Before copy ships, it should be true, clear, and something a skeptical engineer would respect. If it reads like a generic SaaS landing page, it is off-brand.
